Empire State of Mind, But

New York City boasts itself as a city that never sleeps. The subway runs all night long. Songs like Jay-Z’s Empire State of Mind paint the city as a place with endless possibilities saying, “In New York, concrete jungle where dreams are made of, there’s nothing you can’t do.” Frank Sinatra’s rendition of the classic song New York, New York describes its allure in the line, “I want to be a part of it.” It is, without a doubt, a city with a lot to offer, but taking advantage of those opportunities at least requires having access to them. For New York City’s wheelchair users, lack of full access to things like public transportation, restaurants and theaters can prevent them from experiencing the heart of the city’s culture.

As you can see, getting around New York City in a wheelchair is not as easy as the city advertises.

Amy Meisner-Threet, who started using a wheelchair over ten years ago, finds these limitations frustrating. “It can make you say I’m just staying home today,” she said. To make things easier on herself, she stays in her neighborhood most of the time, and when she does travel, she tries to avoid rush hour, but even when her journey goes smoothly in terms of logistics, she still has to deal with rude reactions from people. “One day I got on a bus on Broadway in Manhattan and people were yelling, ‘Why do we have to wait for her? Why does the whole world have to stop for her?’” she said. This attitude contributes to the stress of commuting. Vernita Worrell, who has used a wheelchair for most of her life, generally avoids public transit. She prefers to take Access-A-Ride, though she calls it “Ac-Stress-A-Ride” because it’s stressful, and requires a 24-hour notice for pick ups. Overall, living in New York City is difficult for even the toughest residents, and the difficulty increases if you are living with a disability.
